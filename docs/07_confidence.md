# 07_confidence_public.md
**Corruptibility Index (CI) – Public Explanation of the Confidence Model**  
**High-Level, Non-Technical Summary**

---

## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document describes, in plain language, how the Corruptibility Index (CI) considers the **reliability of information** when generating a score.

It intentionally avoids:

- internal scoring formulas  
- quantitative confidence levels  
- tier definitions  
- proprietary adjustment rules  
- model-specific decision logic  

The goal is to provide transparency without exposing intellectual property.

---

## 2. Why Confidence Matters

Not all public information carries the same level of reliability.

Different sources vary in:

- accuracy  
- verification standards  
- transparency  
- editorial oversight  
- susceptibility to error  

A robust scoring system must account for this.

The CI incorporates a confidence layer to ensure:

- more reliable sources have stronger influence  
- less reliable or unverified information has limited impact  
- patterns supported by multiple sources are treated with greater consistency  
- the overall score remains fair, stable, and grounded in trustworthiness  

---

## 3. What Confidence Means in CI

Think of “confidence” as the model’s way of asking:

> “How much trust should we place in this particular piece of information?”

This is not a judgment of:

- political bias  
- media legitimacy  
- institutional credibility  

Instead, it reflects **research-backed best practices** for handling heterogeneous public records.

---

## 4. How CI Considers Source Reliability (Conceptual Only)

Without revealing internal details, CI’s confidence system broadly considers:

### **1. Type of source**  
Some public sources have higher verification standards than others.

### **2. Documentation quality**  
Clear, structured information is more reliable than vague or ambiguous statements.

### **3. Consistency across sources**  
When multiple independent sources agree, confidence rises.

### **4. Ambiguity or uncertainty**  
If information is unclear or partially supported, CI treats it conservatively.

### **5. Context and traceability**  
Information with clear provenance is treated differently from unverifiable claims.

These principles ensure fairness and balance.

---

## 5. Why CI Uses a Confidence Layer

The confidence layer prevents:

- **overreaction to low-quality information**  
- **inflated risk signals** from unverified reports  
- **penalizing individuals unfairly** due to incomplete or ambiguous data  
- **system vulnerability to misinformation or unusual reporting spikes**  

It also ensures that:

- the score changes smoothly as information evolves  
- strong signals remain meaningful  
- noise is reduced  
- the final output reflects **quality**, not just **quantity**, of public data  

---

## 6. How Confidence Affects Scoring (Public Summary)

While the internal math is proprietary, CI conceptually does the following:

- Highly reliable information has stronger influence on the score.  
- Moderately reliable information contributes cautiously.  
- Weak or unverified information contributes minimally or not at all.  
- Conflicting information is evaluated conservatively.  
- Multiple corroborating sources increase interpretive strength.  

This mimics risk-scoring systems in:

- finance  
- compliance  
- academic research  
- investigative analytics  

---

## 7. What CI Does *Not* Do

CI does **not**:

- label any source as “good” or “bad”  
- make political value judgments  
- rely on rumors or anonymous claims  
- interpret intent  
- determine truth or falsity  

CI simply uses a **responsible weighting philosophy** to reflect real-world variation in information reliability.

---

## 8. Transparency Without Revealing Proprietary Logic

The public receives:

- category descriptions  
- general confidence principles  
- explanations of the model’s intent  

The internal system retains:

- formula details  
- numerical values  
- adjustment rules  
- source-tier definitions  
- cross-source blending mechanisms  

This protects CI from manipulation while ensuring its operation is understandable.
